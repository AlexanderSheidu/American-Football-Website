American Football facts

American Football facts (referred to simply as football in the United States and Canada), alternatively known as gridiron football, is a team sport, in which two teams of eleven players on a rectangular field with goalposts at each end play against each other.

How American Football is played

Offense and defense

  1. The offensive team is the team with possession of the oval-shaped football, attempts to advance down the field by running with the ball or passing it
  2. The defense, the team without possession of the ball, aims to stop the offense's advance and to take control of the ball for themselves
  3. A safety is a scoring play that results in a team getting 2 points on the scoreboard. It can occur if the offense records a penalty in their own end zone, a ball carrier gets tackled in their own end zone after having the ball outside of the end zone at any point during the play, or a fumble or other loose ball gets knocked out of the back of the possessing team's end zone, or out of bounds in the end zone.
  4. Also known as a point-after-touchdown (PAT), it is a scoring opportunity in American football that takes place immediately after a touchdown. The extra point allows a team to earn an additional point, bringing the total score for the scoring play to 7 points
  5. In a two-point conversion attempt, the team that just scored must run a play from scrimmage close to the opponent's goal line and advance the ball across the goal line in the same manner as if they were scoring a touchdown. If the team succeeds, it earns two additional points on top of the six points for the touchdown, for a total of eight points
  6. A touchdown is a scoring play in gridiron football. Whether running, passing, returning a kickoff or punt, or recovering a turnover, a team scores a touchdown by advancing the ball into the opponent's end zone.
  7. A kickoff is a method of starting a drive in gridiron football. Typically, a kickoff consists of one team - the kicking team - kicking the ball to the opposing team - the receiving team
  8. Onside Kick: An onside kick is a strategic move used in American football during a kickoff. The objective is for the kicking team to regain possession of the ball instead of kicking it far downfield to the receiving team.

Advance

The offense must advance at least ten yards in four downs or plays

  • if they fail, they turn over the football to the defense
  • but if they succeed, they are given a new set of four downs to continue the drive.
  • Points are scored primarily by advancing the ball into the opposing team's end zone for a touchdown or kicking the ball through the opponent's goalposts for a field goal.
  • Longest Field Goal in College Football: The longest field goal made in college football history was 69 yards long. The kick was made by Abilene Christian University's kicker, Ove Johansson, on October 16, 1976.
  • Longest Field Goal in NFL: The longest field goal in NFL history is 66 yards, made by Justin Tucker of the Baltimore Ravens.
  • American football is a hodgepodge that developed from two sports: rugby and soccer.
  • College and professional games last 60 minutes, divided into four quarters of 15 minutes. The plays have frequent stops, so a game might last 2.5-3 hours or more.
  • The first game of football was played between Rutgers and Princeton colleges on November 6, 1869.

The team with the most points at the end of a game wins.
